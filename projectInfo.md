What Customers Care About Most
Based on the competitive landscape and Paul’s experience, these are the top concerns every
visitor has. The site must address each one clearly:
1. “Will this actually work?”
Visitors need proof. Before/after photos, measurable claims (block up to 63% of heat, 99% UV
protection), and customer testimonials are the primary trust-builders. Avoid vague or generic
benefit language.
2. “How much does it cost?”
People won’t find exact pricing on the site, but they need a signal that this is accessible. Use
language like “Free estimates,” “Competitive pricing,” and “Financing available” to keep them
from bouncing. The free estimate CTA is the core conversion mechanism.
3. “Is this person legit?”
This is the #1 conversion killer right now. Paul’s current web presence doesn’t convey
professionalism. The new site must feel polished, load fast, and include real credentials,

Primary Reference: AmericanWindowfilm.com
Paul identified this site as the design benchmark. Here’s what to take from it and where to differ:
What to Emulate
• Structure: Clean Residential / Commercial / Solutions navigation. Solutions organized
by customer problem, not product name.
• Trust Bar: Stats row near the top (100,000+ projects, nationwide, 1,800+ reviews, Inc.
5000). Paul’s version should include: 30+ years (brand), lifetime warranty, 25+ reviews,
3M products.
• Case Studies: Real project photos with brief descriptions. Paul needs to build this
library. Start with placeholder slots.
• Benefits Grid: Icons + short benefit descriptions (heat blocking, glare reduction, privacy,
UV, security, LEED, etc.). Scannable, not text-heavy.
• FAQ Section: Addresses common objections directly on the homepage. Reduces
bounce.
• Persistent CTA: "Request Free Quote" button visible in header, mid-page, and footer at
all times.
Where to Differ
• Scale: AWF is a large national company. Paul’s site should feel local and personal.
Include owner photos, personal bios, “we’re your neighbors” messaging.
• Simplicity: AWF has extensive sub-navigation. Paul’s 5–8 page site should keep
navigation tight. Don’t try to replicate their 30+ page depth.
• Video: If Paul has install videos or customer testimonial clips, feature them prominently.
Short-form video converts extremely well on mobile.
• Tone: AWF is polished-corporate. Paul’s should be polished-local. Friendly, direct, no
jargon.

Design Guidelines
Mobile-First, Non-Negotiable
95% of Paul’s current traffic is mobile. His previous mobile experience was non-functional,
which killed conversions. Every design decision starts with mobile:
• Tap-to-call phone number always visible in mobile header
• Sticky CTA bar on mobile ("Call Now" + "Free Estimate" buttons)
• Forms must be short and thumb-friendly (no dropdowns with 30 options)
• Images optimized for mobile load speed (WebP, lazy loading)
• Text readable without pinch-zoom (minimum 16px body)
• Test on actual iPhones and Android devices, not just Chrome DevTools
Visual Direction
• Feel: Professional, clean, trustworthy. Not flashy or over-designed. Think: the kind of site
where you’d feel comfortable giving someone access to your home.
• Color: Pull from CoolVu’s brand palette. Blues and whites communicate trust and
cleanliness. Use warm accent colors sparingly for CTAs and key highlights.
• Photography: Real installation photos are the #1 priority. Supplement with CoolVu
corporate imagery only where gaps exist. No AI-generated images. No obvious stock
photos.
• Typography: Clean, modern sans-serif. High readability. Good contrast ratios for
accessibility.
• Whitespace: Generous. Let content breathe. Cramped layouts feel untrustworthy.
Brand Voice & Copywriting
• Tone: Confident but not salesy. Helpful, knowledgeable, local. Paul is a neighbor who
happens to be an expert.
• Headlines: Benefit-driven, not feature-driven. “Keep Your Home Cooler Without
Replacing Your Windows” beats “Solar Window Film Installation.”
• CTAs: Action-oriented and low-pressure. “Get Your Free Estimate” or “Schedule a Free
Consultation” — never “Buy Now.”
• Proof: Use specific numbers over vague claims. “Blocks up to 63% of solar heat” beats
“reduces heat significantly.”

Conversion Strategy
The entire purpose of this site is lead generation. Every page should funnel visitors toward one
of two actions:
Primary Conversion: Free Estimate Request (Form)
Keep the form short. Name, phone, email, zip code, and a brief project description. Nothing
else. Every additional field reduces submissions.
Secondary Conversion: Phone Call
Tap-to-call must be visible on every screen, especially mobile. Paul is a one-man operation who
closes on the phone — making it easy to call is critical.
Conversion-Boosting Elements
• Social Proof: Google reviews widget or testimonial carousel near every CTA
• Urgency/Relevance: Seasonal messaging (“Beat the summer heat”) and event tie-ins
(home show promo)
• Risk Reversal: Lifetime warranty badge, free estimate language, “no obligation”
• Speed: Page load speed directly impacts conversion. Optimize aggressively.
• Trust Badges: 3M, CoolVu, IWFA, Impact Security logos above the fold
